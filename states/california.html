Statute of Limitations for Credit Card Debt in California

Credit card debt in California is governed by the statute of limitations: 4 (written) / 4 (open) years. After the SOL runs, the creditor or junk-debt buyer can still try to collect, but they cannot obtain a valid judgment if you raise the SOL defense in a timely answer.

  • Clock start: Usually the date of last activity (payment or charge) on the account. State-by-state variation exists.
  • Reset risk: Any acknowledgment, partial payment, or promise to pay can restart the SOL in some California courts.
  • Zombie debt: After SOL, debt buyers (Midland, LVNV, Portfolio Recovery) still file suits hoping you default. Appear and raise the SOL.

California Credit Card Debt vs. Income: Breaking Point

Credit counselors, bankruptcy attorneys, and the CFPB all use similar rules of thumb to identify structural debt distress:

  • CC debt > 15% of gross annual income: serious warning. For California, that threshold is $13,725.
  • Monthly minimums > 20% of take-home: means-test / DMP territory.
  • Back-end DTI > 43%: disqualifies most conventional / FHA mortgages and signals bankruptcy territory.

The average California card-holder carrying $6,600 at a 24% APR pays roughly $132/month just in interest before principal reduction.

Options When CC Debt Overwhelms Income in California

  1. Hardship program (direct with issuer). Free; no credit damage beyond existing. Most major issuers offer 6-12 month APR reduction or forbearance.
  2. Nonprofit credit counseling (DMP). Consolidates payments, no new debt. Small monthly admin fee. See California options.
  3. Debt settlement. 40-60% payoffs typical; heavy credit damage; may trigger 1099-C at $3,300+ forgiveness. See California 1099-C treatment.
  4. Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Complete discharge of unsecured CC debt; means test applies to California median income.
  5. Chapter 13 bankruptcy. 3- or 5-year plan; 0-100% repayment to unsecured depending on disposable income.
